Salmon of Knowledge

In Irish mythology, there was a special salmon that held all the wisdom of the world, as it had eaten nine magical hazelnuts that fell into the Well of Wisdom. A prophecy stated that the first person to eat the fish would gain this knowledge. The poet Finnegas spent years trying to catch this Salmon of Knowledge. He finally caught it and, not wanting the knowledge for himself, gave it to his young apprentice, Fionn mac Cumhaill, to cook, with strict instructions not to eat a single bite.
While Fionn was cooking the fish, a drop of hot oil spat onto his thumb. Instinctively, Fionn put his thumb in his mouth to cool the burn, accidentally ingesting a tiny piece of the salmon's skin. When Fionn brought the meal to his master, Finnegas saw a new light of wisdom in the boy's eyes and knew the prophecy was fulfilled. Finnegas had Fionn eat the rest of the salmon, and from that day on, Fionn could gain immense wisdom and foresight simply by biting his thumb.

Adam and Eve

According to the Book of Genesis, God created the first man, Adam, and placed him in a paradise called the Garden of Eden. To provide him with a companion, God created the first woman, Eve, from one of Adam's ribs. They lived in a state of innocence, with only one command from God: they were forbidden to eat the fruit from the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil. However, a serpent tempted Eve, convincing her that the fruit would make her like God. Eve ate the fruit and gave some to Adam, who also ate it, marking the first act of human disobedience.
Immediately after eating the fruit, Adam and Eve's eyes were opened, and they realized they were naked, feeling shame for the first time. When God confronted them, they blamed each other and the serpent. As a consequence, God cursed the serpent, ordained that Eve would experience pain in childbirth, and decreed that Adam would have to toil and work the land for food. For their transgression, known as the "Fall of Man," God banished Adam and Eve from the Garden of Eden, introducing sin, suffering, and mortality into the world.
